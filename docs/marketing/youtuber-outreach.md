# Conjoyn — Drone-YouTuber / Creator Outreach Kit

> Seeding free licenses to drone-workflow creators is one of the highest-leverage moves for a solo dev:
> one workflow video that ranks for "DJI editing workflow" can outperform months of forum posts, and each
> creator who adopts Conjoyn becomes a recurring distribution channel. The whole game is being *useful and
> low-pressure* — give value, ask nothing, let the tool sell itself.

---

## Who to target

- **Mid-size, on-topic > huge & generic.** A 15k-subscriber channel about *DJI footage workflow / editing*
  converts better than a 2M-subscriber general-tech channel. Relevance beats reach.
- Channels that already make: "how I edit drone footage," "DJI [model] tips," "speed up your drone
  workflow," telemetry/mapping, real-estate or inspection drone content.
- Also: drone bloggers, newsletter writers, and Mac-software reviewers in the same niche.
- Build a simple list (10–20 names) with: channel, subscriber range, the specific video that shows
  they'd care, and their contact (business email in the About tab, or a comment/DM).

---

## Outreach principles

1. **Give, don't ask.** Lead with a free license, no strings. Don't demand a video.
2. **Prove you watch them.** Reference a *specific* video — generic blasts get ignored/marked spam.
3. **Make the relevance obvious in one line.** They split files too; you solve it.
4. **Be honest it's yours and that it's small.** Solo-dev authenticity plays well with this crowd.
5. **One short follow-up max.** Then move on — no pestering.
6. **Disclosure:** if a creator does cover it, a free license in exchange for an *honest* review is fine,
   but they should disclose it per their platform's rules. Never ask for a fake-positive review.

> **Subject:** Free license for [Channel] — a Mac tool for split DJI files
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I watched your "[specific video title]" — [one genuine, specific sentence proving you actually saw it,

>

> flights into ~4 GB files, and rejoining them by hand is tedious. Conjoyn auto-detects which segments

> I'd love to just **give you a free license** — no ask, no strings. If it saves you time, great; if you
> ever feel like mentioning it to your audience, even better, but genuinely no pressure either way. I'd
> mostly love your honest take on where it falls short.
>
> Want me to send a code over? Free trial's here too if you'd rather poke at it first: [link].
>
> Cheers,
> [Name] — solo dev, Conjoyn
> [site] · [email]

## If they say yes — make it effortless for them

- Send the license **immediately**, plus a 2-line "what makes it different" so they don't have to dig
  (lossless + auto-grouping + **SRT telemetry** + watch-folder).
- Offer the **demo video, screenshots, and b-roll** they can drop into a review (link the demo-video assets).
- Offer a **unique discount/affiliate code** for their audience — gives them a reason to mention it and lets
  you attribute the traffic.
- Be available for questions and ship fixes fast — a creator who feels heard becomes a repeat advocate.
- Ask (don't require) that they disclose the free license, per FTC / platform norms.

---

## What NOT to do

- ❌ Mass-blast an identical email to 200 channels.
- ❌ Lead with "will you make a video about my app?"
- ❌ Pay for a guaranteed-positive review (kills credibility, breaks platform rules).
- ❌ Follow up more than once.
- ❌ Target creators whose audience doesn't shoot DJI/Mac — reach without relevance is wasted.

## Track it

Keep a tiny sheet: creator · contacted (date) · responded? · license sent? · covered? · code/affiliate ·
installs attributed. After ~20 outreaches you'll see which creator *type* converts — then go deeper there.
